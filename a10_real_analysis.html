Assignment 10 — Manipulation Analysis

Target Grade: B

Analysis

The deceptive chart was designed to make the calorie values in the McDonald’s breakfast dataset appear more extreme and more meaningful than they really are. One of the main techniques used was reordering the items by calorie value. In the original dataset, the breakfast items are simply different menu items, not a time series or a natural sequence. By sorting them from lower calorie to higher calorie and then plotting them in that order, the chart creates the impression of a steady upward trend. This makes the viewer more likely to read the visualization as evidence of escalation, even though the pattern was created by the way the data was arranged.

A second deceptive technique was the truncated y-axis. Instead of starting at zero, the deceptive chart begins much higher, which visually stretches out the differences between items. This makes the gap between 250 calories and 450 calories feel much larger than it would in a more honest chart. A third technique was the use of a red color scale and a legend framed as calorie risk. This encourages the viewer to associate higher calorie values with danger or severity, even though the dataset itself is only reporting calorie counts. The loaded title and annotation on the highest point add to that framing by directing attention toward the most extreme value.

In contrast, the ethical chart communicates something much more limited and accurate. It shows the same calorie values, but it keeps the original item labels, uses a neutral blue color, and starts the y-axis at zero. Because of that, the viewer sees the chart as a simple comparison of menu items rather than a dramatic pattern. The ethical version still allows the data to be explored through tooltips, but it does not push the viewer toward a specific emotional response. Overall, the comparison shows that visual framing can strongly influence interpretation even when the underlying data stays exactly the same.
